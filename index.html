Shark Tooth Island A field guide to the Cape Fear's prehistoric shore

An uninhabited dredge spoil island in the Cape Fear River — one of the most unusual fossil hunting destinations on the East Coast. No admission. No crowds. Forty million years of marine history, revealed by the tide.

Getting there

Getting there and
doing it right

There's no bridge, no ferry, no launch from the island itself. You can get there by kayak, SUP, or a unique GoBoat expedition. The experience rewards preparation — the right tide, the right gear, enough time to actually hunt.

Access

  • Boat or kayak only — no land access from shore
  • River Road Park is the most common launch point
  • Short paddle from the park to the island cluster
  • Schedule your launch around low tide, not convenience
  • Allow 3–4 hours minimum for a real hunt
  • Check Cape Fear River tide tables, not beach tables

What to bring

  • Water shoes or old sneakers — the sediment is rough
  • A mesh strainer or sifter (old colander works)
  • Sun protection — there's no shade on the island
  • Bug spray, especially spring and fall mornings
  • A small dry bag for your phone and finds
  • More water than you think you need

How to search

  • Work the waterline at low tide — fossils concentrate there
  • Scoop sediment and sift rather than surface scanning
  • Look for the triangular silhouette and flat, smooth surface
  • Black is the color — ignore brown, tan, or white fragments
  • Dense and heavy is a good sign; shell is light
  • Explore the other islands in the cluster — Keg Island too

Leave it right

  • Pack out everything you bring in
  • Fossil collecting for personal use is generally permitted
  • Respect the wildlife — fiddler crabs, shorebirds, and gators are all present
  • Uninhabited doesn't mean unmanaged — tread carefully
When to go

Timing is everything —
here's why

The single biggest factor in a successful hunt isn't technique or luck. It's tide. Low tide exposes sediment that's submerged the rest of the day. A 1–2 hour window around dead low is prime.

Optimal window
Low tide ± 1 hr

Dead low is your peak window. Start arriving one hour before low and plan to stay until one hour after. The best spots are only accessible at true low.

Tide differential
Higher = better hunt

A 4–5 foot tidal range exposes far more island than a 2-foot range. Check the Cape Fear River tide tables, not just the beach charts — they differ.

Season
Spring and fall

Milder temperatures, manageable bugs, lower boat traffic. Summer works but heat and insects make extended hunts uncomfortable. Winter low tides can be exceptional but access gets tricky.

After dredging
Best windows of all

Recent dredge activity on the Cape Fear can dramatically improve a season. Fresh material gets deposited, tides sort it, and finds spike. Worth tracking Corps of Engineers activity in the area.

How it formed

Human engineering meets
ancient geology

Shark Tooth Island didn't exist until the late 1800s. The Army Corps of Engineers dredged the Cape Fear River to allow large cargo vessels to reach Wilmington — and in doing so, pulled millions of years of buried marine sediment to the surface. Fossilized teeth, bone, and shell from four geological epochs were pumped out and deposited as "spoil," forming new islands. Every tidal cycle erodes fine sediment, concentrating denser material on the surface. What the dredge created, the tide continues to reveal.

Geological record

Four epochs in
one island

The dredged sediment contains fossils from four distinct geological epochs — a compressed record of 50 million years of marine life.

Field guide

What to look for,
what to expect

Almost every fossil on this island is black — the result of mineral replacement over millions of years. Train your eye to recognize the triangular silhouette and smooth, flat enamel surface. In a pile of shell and rock, teeth have a different weight and density that you'll start to feel.

Sand tiger shark teeth
Common

Long, narrow, needle-like with pronounced cusps at the base. Usually 1–1.5 inches. The most frequently found tooth here — excellent for beginners learning what fossilized teeth look like.

Mackerel shark teeth
Common

Broader, flatter, with fine serrations along both edges. Triangular profile. Ancestors of the modern great white shark. Ranges 1–3 inches. The serrations are the tell.

Megalodon teeth
Rare

Large, thick, heavily serrated. Typically 3–7 inches. Deep black, near-mirror enamel on the crown surface. Finding one is a genuine discovery. Most serious hunters have found only a handful in their lives.
