Sea levels rise and fall dramatically as glaciers advance and retreat. The Cape Fear River cuts deep into the coastal plain during low sea stands, reworking older sediment — and the fossils within it — into new deposits.

Final Megalodon
5.3 – 2.6 Ma

Otodus megalodon goes extinct. The exact cause is debated — cooling oceans, collapse of prey populations, competition from great white sharks, or a combination of all three. The last known specimens date to roughly 3.6 Ma. What you find on the island is all that remains.

Neogene · Miocene
Peak Megalodon
23 – 5.3 Ma

The undisputed apex of megalodon's reign. Warm, shallow seas cover much of what is now the Carolina coastal plain. Otodus megalodon reaches its maximum size — estimates range from 50 to 65 feet — hunting large baleen whales in productive coastal waters. Most teeth found on Shark Tooth Island originate from this epoch.

Most local finds Warm shallow seas Whale prey abundant Peak body size

Paleogene · Oligocene
Megalodon Emerges
33.9 – 23 Ma

Otodus megalodon first appears in the fossil record, evolving from the earlier Otodus obliquus lineage. Early specimens are smaller than their later descendants. The Atlantic coastline resembles modern-day Florida — a broad carbonate platform fronting a warm, shallow sea.

Paleogene · Eocene
Ancient Seas
56 – 33.9 Ma

The warmest sustained period of the Cenozoic. Much of the Carolina coastal plain is fully submerged beneath a shallow, tropical sea. The Otodus obliquus shark — megalodon's predecessor — is the apex predator. Occasional Eocene teeth surface in Cape Fear sediment.

Mesozoic · Late Cretaceous
Before Sharks Ruled
100 – 66 Ma

The Western Interior Seaway splits North America. Marine reptiles — mosasaurs and plesiosaurs — dominate. Sharks exist but are smaller and more diverse. The mass extinction event at 66 Ma reshapes ocean ecosystems and clears the path for the lamniform lineage that will eventually produce megalodon.
