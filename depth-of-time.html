Geological record

Depth of Time —
50 million years in one island

The dredged sediment of Shark Tooth Island contains fossils from four distinct geological epochs. Each layer tells a different story of what the Cape Fear region looked like, what lived here, and what ended up preserved in stone.

56 – 33 million years ago

Eocene

The warmest period in the Cenozoic. North Carolina sat beneath a warm, shallow subtropical sea. The coastline was far inland of where it is today. Primitive sharks, rays, and bony fish dominated these waters. Early whale ancestors were just beginning to transition from land to sea.

What you'll find from this era

Small sand tiger teeth, ray crushing plates, and occasional bony fish vertebrae. Teeth from this period tend to be smaller and lighter in color — dark brown to amber rather than jet black. Striatolamia (sand tiger ancestor) teeth are the most common Eocene find here.

Striatolamia teeth Ray plates Bony fish vertebrae Shark vertebrae
33 – 23 million years ago

Oligocene

Global cooling reshaped ocean circulation. The Drake Passage opened between South America and Antarctica, establishing the Antarctic Circumpolar Current. Sea levels dropped. Early toothed whales and baleen whales appeared in these changing waters.

What you'll find from this era

Transitional shark teeth — larger than Eocene specimens but not yet the massive forms of the Miocene. Early whale ear bones (tympanic bullae) occasionally surface. This is the period when the megalodon lineage was emerging.

Early Otodus teeth Whale ear bones Larger sand tigers Mako ancestors
23 – 5 million years ago

Miocene

The golden age for Shark Tooth Island. Warm seas returned, and the megalodon reached its peak dominance as the largest predatory shark that ever lived. The Cape Fear region was a productive marine environment with abundant prey — whales, dolphins, sea turtles, and large fish. This is where most of the island's signature finds come from.

What you'll find from this era

The majority of significant teeth on the island date to the Miocene. Megalodon teeth in the 2–5 inch range, great white ancestors (Cosmopolitodus), large mako teeth, and whale bone fragments. Teeth from this period are typically jet black with excellent enamel preservation.

Megalodon teeth Great white ancestors Mako teeth Whale bone Sea turtle shell
5 – 2.5 million years ago

Pliocene

The twilight of the megalodon. Cooling oceans, shifting prey migrations, and the rise of modern great white sharks and orcas squeezed the giant predator out of its niche. By the end of the Pliocene, Otodus megalodon was extinct. Modern shark species — the ones still swimming today — became established.

What you'll find from this era

Late-stage megalodon teeth (often showing signs of nutritional stress or reduced size), modern great white teeth, and an abundance of smaller species. The transition from ancient to modern marine fauna is visible in the fossil record here.

Late megalodon Modern great white Bull shark teeth Dolphin teeth Stingray barbs
